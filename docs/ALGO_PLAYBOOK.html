How a pick earns its number

A pick's score is the credibility of the people standing behind it: 10 for an unknown name, up past 100 when ranked winners lead the way. This is the complete story of where that number comes from, in plain betting language.

Current as of July 23, 2026 · reflects the MLB in-sport rework (chapter 3, "The MLB rule": baseball picks are scored from the MLB-only capper ranking, 20 graded MLB bets required to rank, joiners chip a quarter instead of a half, and the v4-era MLB record was restated under the new rules with 74 picks retired), plus the real conviction curve (chapter 6: the chunked reveal is retired; backer and fade points show at their true timestamps with netted best-backer steps, and only the four bonus components surface at seeded random moments, at least 3 hours before start), plus the Covers tracking expansion (consensus pick leaders join the contest players, roughly the top 300 members by units), plus the Wilson percentile rework (capper rank drives every point, the flat base and flat sport bonus are gone, sources no longer score, full influence reserved for the top 1 percent of the ranking), the break-even gate and the hard zero (a win rate of 49 percent or below contributes nothing at all), the money gate (a ledger clearly below breakeven at flat stakes pins a capper to the flat 10 until it recovers), instant grading (every backer's record and the rankings update the moment games go final), the CA line lock at T-60 (the official line locks 1 hour before start, the bet-placed moment; the old reach-gold lock is retired), the pregame flip (until a game actually begins, the tracked bet follows whichever side leads, swapping at the locked line), a spelled-out answer to when a bet is tracked (chapter 7), and the true-start freeze (a tracked score follows the board until the game actually goes live, so listed start times that run late, tennis especially, cannot freeze or void a bet early)
01

The big picture

The score answers one question: how much verified evidence stands behind this side of this game today? Not hype, not follower counts, not which chat room it appeared in. Evidence means graded results in our own ledger.

Three rules shape everything underneath:

RuleWhat it means
Credibility is earnedEvery capper, expert, wallet, and source starts near zero and earns points only through picks we graded ourselves. A famous handle walks in as a credible newcomer, never as a star.
Points are never subtractedNothing ever removes points from a pick. When a losing capper backs a side, the penalty lands as bonus points on the opposite side instead (chapter 3, fade points).
One scale everywhereThe picks list, the game popup, the detail page, and the history archive all show the same number for the same pick. 100 or more is gold. 75 to 99 is silver.
There is no base anymore. The old flat 45 that every pick started with is gone. It never carried information, and worse, it let weak picks sit closer to the tiers than they deserved. Now a pick from a nobody is worth 10, and everything above that has to be earned by the ranked people backing it. A number can only be big because somebody credible made it big.
02

Where picks come from

Four systems feed the board. All of them are free to run, and all of them are held to the same standard: we grade every pick ourselves against the final score. Nobody gets to bring their own record.

SourceWhoHow often we checkNotes
DiscordCappers posting in the tracked channelsInstantly, the moment a message postsAn AI reader extracts the pick, the team, and the capper name from each message.
Action NetworkAbout 52 public expertsEvery 10 minutes during the dayTheir site record is shown on profiles for context but never counts toward scoring.
PolymarketTop 50 traders by lifetime profitEvery 15 minutesReal money positions of 200 dollars or more on game markets count as picks. Bet size versus their usual is logged for future study.
CoversKing of Covers contest players plus the top of the all-time consensus pick leaders board (roughly the top 300 members by units)Every 30 minutesContest picks lock at game start, so pending picks are provably pregame. Leaders' site records are shown for context but never count toward scoring.

House rules for every source

Pregame only. A pick counts for the board only if the source's own timestamp is before the game started. Anything placed mid game is kept for the capper's record but never touches the board.

One person, one voice. If the same capper shows up on the same side of the same game from two systems, that is one pick with two receipts, not two picks. The systems can never stack points by echoing each other.

03

The score recipe

Seven ingredients, all additive. The width of each bar shows how much of the total that ingredient can contribute at most.

Best backer0 to 80

The engine. Every capper is ranked on one board by the Wilson score (chapter 4), and their percentile position decides what their picks are worth: the #1 capper delivers 80 points just by posting, a top 1 percent capper 70 to 80, a mid-pack capper around 15, a bottom-quarter capper nothing. The strongest ranked person on the pick sets this number. Even the very best name cannot reach gold alone; a gold needs corroboration.

A pick whose capper we cannot identify, or whose capper has no graded record yet, is worth a flat 10. Sources are not people: the Action Network pipe, the Discord rooms, the Polymarket firehose earn nothing as entities anymore. Only the individuals inside them do.

Backer stackopen, tapered hard

What the other backers chip in: half of their own worth, tapering by pairs, and only if they have 12 or more graded bets with us. MLB is stricter: a quarter of their worth, and only backers with 20 or more graded MLB bets count (see The MLB rule below). Full detail in chapter 5.

Sport rank bonus0, 10, or 20

Specialists get extra. Each sport keeps its own Wilson ranking, and when the pick's best backer is that sport's #1 or top 5 percent, the pick gets +20. Top 25 percent gets +10. This one has no volume cap by design: a 5-0 tennis specialist sitting on top of the tennis board collects the full bonus.

Market signals0 to 8

Three small, independent checks against real markets: prediction markets pricing this side higher than the sportsbook implies (up to 5), the betting line having moved toward this side since morning (2), and 65 percent or more of public tickets sitting on the other side (1). Deliberately small caps for now. The full values are logged so their weights can be re-fit on evidence later.

Side lean0 to 5

The replacement for the old automatic home team bonus. Each night the system measures whether home or away sides have actually been more profitable per sport over the last 120 days (minimum 100 graded picks each way) and leans that direction. Tennis and golf are excluded because their home and away labels are just listing order.

Fade points0 to 8, plus offset

When a capper on the official fade list (chapter 4) backs one side, the opposite side receives 3 to 8 bonus points. Scaled by how much we have seen that capper in this sport, so a losing MLB record fades MLB picks but says almost nothing about their rare NBA pick. If a proven capper is standing on the faded side, that side gets an offsetting bonus too. Nothing is ever taken away from anyone.

The totals gate. One extra check for over/under picks only: a pick cannot be tracked as gold if its best backer has a losing record specifically on totals. Board totals have historically bled money, so gold totals require a backer who has actually won them.
The MLB rule (July 23). Baseball gets its own, stricter version of the first two ingredients, because the data demanded it. Over 21 days MLB golds went 77-83 while every other sport combined won over 63 percent, and the reason was structural: a good record in other sports simply did not carry into baseball (ranked cappers hit 61.7 percent everywhere else but only 55.1 in MLB), while the people with a real MLB track record were winning 60.5 percent. Baseball also floods the board: fifteen games a day meant crowds of mediocre backers could stack a pick past gold on volume alone, and the most crowded MLB picks won just 43.9 percent. So for MLB only: a backer's points come from the MLB-only ranking, not the overall board. Fewer than 20 graded MLB bets means the flat 10, no matter how good their tennis record looks. And joiners chip in a quarter of their worth instead of a half, qualified MLB backers only, so a crowd can never mint a gold by itself. The result on replay: about half the MLB golds of the past two weeks would never have existed, and the ones that survive were backed by people who have actually proven it in baseball. Other sports can be moved onto this same rule later if their numbers show the same pattern; the admin Cappers tab now shows every sport's own ladder for exactly that call.
04

How a capper earns points

Every graded pick lands in a permanent ledger that survives the daily reset, for every backer on the pick, the moment the game goes final. The re-rank happens right then too: profiles, the leaderboard, and every pick scored from that moment use the current record (with a full sweep each night at 5:20am as the backstop). The ranking is one number with a long pedigree: the Wilson score, the same math Reddit uses to rank comments so that 900 upvotes out of 1,000 beats 3 out of 3.

1. The worst-case win rate

The Wilson score asks: given this record, what is the LOWEST the capper's true win rate could plausibly be? We use the cautious 99 percent version. A 7-0 heater has a great face but a thin file, so its worst case is modest. An 85-49 grind over 134 bets has a worst case that is genuinely strong. That is why MidwestMike ranks #1 overall while perfect little records sit below him. Volume is not a bonus; it is the proof that makes the number trustworthy.

2. One board, every capper

Everyone with at least one graded decision goes into a single ranking: Discord cappers, Action Network experts, Polymarket wallets, Covers contest players, all treated identically. Your percentile position on that board maps to points through the ladder:

Where you rankYour picks are worth
Top 1%80 down to 70
1 to 5%67 down to 61
5 to 15%60 down to 51
15 to 25%50 down to 41
25 to 35%40 down to 31
35 to 45%30 down to 21
45 to 75%20 down to 11
Bottom 25%0
Win rate 49 percent or below, any rank0
No graded record / unknown name10 flat

Points slide inside each band, so the top of the 5-15 band is worth 60 and the bottom of it 51. Higher rank always means more points. There is one deliberate cliff: the bottom of the top 1 percent band is 76, and the band below it starts at 67. Full influence is reserved for the very top of the board.

3. Thin records are capped, not banned

A hot 7-0 can rank high on the board, but what its picks can collect is capped until the sample earns trust: under 10 graded decisions caps at 50 points, 10 to 29 caps at 70, and 30 or more collects the full ladder value. Nobody golds a pick off a lucky week.

4. The break-even gate

Ranking high is evidence you exist; handing out points also requires evidence you win. The Wilson score rewards volume, so a capper with a long losing record can still out-rank a crowd of thin ones. The gate stops that from mattering: a capper's win rate (shrunk toward the coin flip on small samples) must clear 50 percent for their backing to be worth more than the unknown-name 10, and full value returns at 53 percent, roughly break-even at standard juice. Between the two it scales smoothly.

And below the gate sits the hard zero: a raw win rate of 49 percent or under contributes nothing. Not the 10, no chip-in, and no sport rank bonus even where that capper tops a sport board. A demonstrated loser is worth less than an unknown name; the flat 10 is reserved for records we have not seen yet, not records we have seen lose.

4b. The money gate (July 13)

Win rate ignores price. A capper who lives on heavy favorites can hit 61 percent and still lose money at flat stakes, because favorites pay little when they win and cost full price when they lose. The win-rate gate cannot see that person, so a second gate reads the ledger itself: a capper's smoothed flat-stake ROI (their total units, steadied the same way the win-rate gate steadies small samples) must be at breakeven or better for full value.

Below breakeven their value slides down smoothly, and once the hole is deep enough (about minus five percent smoothed) they pin to the flat 10: no chip-ins onto other picks, no sport rank bonus, nothing above what an unknown name gets, until wins pull the ledger back up. The scale is deliberate. Being $40 down over 200 picks is normal variance and keeps most of its value, while a ledger down ten percent of everything it ever staked pins hard. Whichever gate is stricter, the win-rate one or the money one, is the one that binds. Rankings and bands stay untouched, and the fade rules stay on win rate; the money gate only controls what a capper's backing is worth. Release is automatic, since every graded slate recomputes the ledger.

5. The fade list

Being ranked low is not enough to get faded; a thin record and a bad record are different things. Fading requires both a bottom-quarter rank and a genuinely losing win rate:

FlagRequirementEffect
FADE WATCHbottom 25%, win% 45 or under, 5+ betsTheir picks contribute nothing.
FADE ACTIVEbottom 25%, win% 40 or under, 15+ betsTheir picks add points to the opposite side.

Sources are no longer cappers

The old system tracked each pipe (Action Network as a whole, each Discord room) as a pseudo-capper that could carry picks by itself. That ended on July 9: the Action Network pipe had quietly pooled enough volume to out-rank every human being we track, and it was minting golds off single unproven backers. Pipes deliver picks; only people earn points.

05

Consensus: the chip-in

The question consensus answers: when several tracked cappers land on the same side, how much extra is that agreement worth? The answer went through two designs in one day, and the second exists because the first failed in public.

The rule

The strongest backer counts in full. Every other backer chips in half of their own worth, with two brakes:

The pair taper. Within each ranking band, the first two extra backers chip in half their worth, the next two a quarter, the two after that an eighth. Agreement is worth something; an echo chamber is not worth an echo chamber's size.

The 12-bet floor. A backer needs 12 or more graded bets with us before their chip-in counts at all. Their own picks still score and they still rank; they just cannot boost anyone else's pick until they have a real file. This one rule is what separates "three proven winners agree" from "a dozen anonymous wallets piled on". (It launched at 20; lowered to 12 the same week so a strong 11-3 name is not silenced as a joiner.)

CaseChip-in
A 70-point capper with 40 graded bets joins+35
A second 70-point capper from the same band joins+35, then the band tapers
A hot 6-1 wallet joins (under 20 bets)0 until they hit 20
An unknown or bottom-quarter capper joins0
A fade-list capper joins0, always (and may fade the pick's opposite side)
Why the floor exists. The first Wilson rescore on July 9 let any ranked backer chip in. Within the hour a tennis pick had collected 22 backers, most of them thin Polymarket wallets riding 5-1 style records, and hit 349 points; the board carried 13 golds, more than the crowding this rework was built to kill. With the pair taper and the 20-bet floor the same board settled to roughly 6 golds, every one led by a top-band capper. Verified on the live data before it shipped.
06

The reveal

Since July 16 the conviction curve is real. Every point lands on the public score at the moment it actually happened: a backer's points step the curve at the exact time their pick came in, and fade points tally the instant the opposite side draws fade-list action. The old chunked climb (show 25, sit, jump 9, then 12) is retired; what members watch IS the conviction building, not a dramatization of it.

What still stays hidden

Two layers of protection remain, and they are enough:

  • Bonus timingThe four formula-shaped components (sport rank bonus, market signals, side lean, sport bonus) each surface at one random moment per pick, always at least 3 hours before game start. A +6 market step at 11:23am cannot be matched to the line move that earned it. The moments are seeded per pick, so the board, the popup, and the curve always agree, and a moment never changes once dealt. A pick born inside the final 3 hours trickles them out within about 20 minutes of arriving, still finishing before start.
  • Backer nettingWhen a stronger backer overtakes, the old best backer folds into the stack at half value, so the curve shows one combined step. A 50-point capper opens the pick at +50; a 90-point capper landing later shows as +65 (their 90, plus the 50 halved to 25, minus the 50 already showing). Nobody can read a single capper's worth off a join.

The public number is simply the true score minus any bonus moments still ahead, so it always reaches the full true score before first pitch, tip, or kick.

Why it changed: the synthetic climb hid the real story and could drift from how the points actually accrued. The real replay is honest about timing where honesty is safe (who backed it, and when), and random only where the timing itself would leak the formula (the bonus components).

07

Gold, silver, and the record

TierLineTreatment
Gold100+The tracked tier. Saved permanently, graded publicly, and it is the record the site stands on. Totals must also pass the totals gate.
Silver75 to 99Styled on the board so strong picks stand out. Never tracked, never part of the public record.
Archive50+Quietly saved into the permanent pick history for research and capper grading. Not a public tier.

Gold is intentionally hard, and under the Wilson engine it takes a top name PLUS corroboration: the #1 capper alone is 80, so even the best record we track needs the sport rank bonus or real chip-ins from other ranked winners to cross 100. A crowd of strangers cannot do it, and neither can one name on its own. In practice that means a handful of golds on a full slate, every one led by a top-band capper with company.

The moment a bet is tracked

A pick becomes a tracked bet the instant its true score crosses 100 (a total also has to clear the totals gate). Not when it posts, not when the reveal finishes, not at game start. The crossing puts the bet on the record and stamps its day:

  • Bet recordedThe pick lands in the permanent gold record. Its price is the game's official line, which is settled separately at the 1-hour mark below; a pick that crosses earlier tracks the market until then, and a pick that crosses later inherits the already-locked number.
  • Day stampedThe bet files under the day the game is actually played. The game's own start time decides the board day, so an overnight post about tomorrow's tennis lands on tomorrow's record, not tonight's.

The bet is priced 1 hour before the game. The game's CA line locks exactly 60 minutes before the listed start (the old reach-gold lock is retired). Whatever the freshest book shows at that moment becomes the official line everywhere, and every tracked bet on that game is priced at it, as if the bet were placed right there. Grading and the P/L math use this frozen number no matter where the market moves afterward. The 1-hour mark follows the real start time, so a schedule slip moves it too.

The score can keep climbing; the price cannot move. Backers who arrive after the crossing still raise the recorded score, right up to the moment the game actually goes live. The listed start time is only an estimate (tennis especially runs late), so the freeze keys on the real first pitch or first serve, and the tracked score follows the board the whole way there. Once the game is live the score is frozen, and once a result is on the row nothing changes it again.

No price, no bet. A moneyline pick with no betting line available from any book is not tracked yet, because there is no P/L truth without a price. The row is written the moment a line shows up, and a pick whose line never materializes is never tracked. Spreads and totals settle at standard juice, so only moneylines wait.

One bet per game, and it follows the leader. If the other side of a game overtakes the tracked pick while the game is still pregame, the bet flips: the old hypothetical bet is removed from the record (pending pregame bets are never public, so nothing visible disappears) and one flat unit goes on the new leader at the same locked 1-hour line. This can happen any time before the real start, however late. If both sides are dead even when the game goes live, both are voided (rare). After the start, nothing flips. For the future real-money mirror, a flip after a position is already open is the case that needs a hedge calculation on the prediction market side to cover the original stake; for the hypothetical P/L it is a clean swap.

The reveal has no say in any of this. Tracking keys on the true score, so a bet is often already recorded while members are still watching its public number climb.

A standing alarm watches the gold tier's trailing 30-day record. If gold ever stops beating the old system's benchmark, that alarm is the signal to recalibrate, and it is checked on real results, not vibes.

08

A day in the life

  • 12:30am ETThe pick cycle opens. Overnight posts count toward the coming day.
  • 4:58amFinal grading pass for late West Coast games, then the daily reset wipes the board. Permanent ledgers (capper history, pick history, gold record) are never touched.
  • 5:00amMorning build: game slate from ESPN, betting lines, the six pick slots per game, then a full re-scan of overnight messages.
  • 5:05amSource refresh: Action Network roster, Polymarket wallet list, Covers contestants.
  • 5:20amThe nightly re-rank. Every capper's Wilson score, board position, ladder points, sport ranks, and fade flags recompute from the full graded ledger. This is the full nightly sweep; during the day the same re-rank also fires the moment any batch of games grades, so results become scoring power right away.
  • All dayDiscord picks land instantly. Action Network polls every 10 minutes, Polymarket every 15, Covers every 30. Every arrival rescores its pick slot and, if needed, starts a reveal.
  • 4:00pmAfternoon line refresh for evening games.
  • T-60: the line lockEach game's CA line locks 1 hour before it starts, and that is the moment the hypothetical bet is placed. Whatever the market shows then becomes the official line: the number the rankings display and every tracked bet is priced at and grades against. Before that a pick shows the morning line as a placeholder; once locked it never moves. The 1-hour mark follows the real start, so a schedule slip moves with it. The tracked side can still flip to the other side of the game any time before the real start (at this same locked line); the score itself keeps tallying until the game actually goes live.
  • Every 5 minLive scores update and finished games get graded. Every backer on a graded pick gets the result on their ledger immediately, and the capper rankings recompute on the spot.

10

Glossary

ML (moneyline)
A bet on who wins the game, no point spread involved. Soccer moneylines have three outcomes; a draw grades both sides as losses.
Spread
A bet on the margin of victory. Brewers -1.5 means they must win by 2 or more.
O/U (over/under, totals)
A bet on combined points scored, over or under a line like 8.5. The line is the number; the juice is the price you pay to bet it (often -110 or -115).
Odds, American
-131 means risk 131 to win 100. +150 means risk 100 to win 150. Negative numbers are favorites.
Units
Profit measured in equal-sized bets at real odds. The site's honest currency for records: it accounts for whether wins came on favorites or underdogs.
ROI (return on investment)
Units won divided by units risked, as a percentage. A +8% ROI capper makes 8 cents per dollar bet over time.
Wilson score
The lower bound of the 99 percent confidence interval on a win rate: the worst the record could plausibly be. The site's one ranking number. Volume raises it; thin perfection does not.
Band
Where a capper's rank falls on the points ladder (top 1%, 1-5%, 5-15%, and so on). The band plus the position inside it decides what their picks are worth.
Volume cap
The lid on what a thin record can collect: under 10 graded decisions caps at 50 points, 10 to 29 at 70, 30 or more uncapped.
Best backer
The strongest ranked capper on a pick. Their ladder points, in full, are the pick's foundation.
Chip-in
What each additional backer adds: half of their own worth, tapered in pairs per band, and only once they have 12 or more graded bets.
Shrunk record (blend)
A capper's ROI after mixing in a skeptical baseline of imaginary break-even picks. Still used for the totals gate and fade routing details.
Source entity
A pipe (Action Network as a whole, a Discord room) formerly tracked as a pseudo-capper. Retired from scoring on July 9; shown in ops tooling only.
Fade
Betting against someone. A fade-active capper's picks add points to the opposite side of whatever they back.
Slot
One of six bettable sides per game the board pre-creates: home ML, away ML, home spread, away spread, over, under. Picks attach to slots.
Mention
One capper backing one slot, from any source. A slot's score recomputes on every new mention.
Reveal (formerly the leak rule)
Chapter 6. Backer and fade points show in real time; the four bonus components each surface at one seeded random moment, at least 3 hours before start. The public number is the true score minus moments still ahead. The old chunked climb is retired.
Conviction curve
The score-over-time chart on a pick's detail page. A real replay: every step sits at the actual timestamp its points arrived (bonus components at their reveal moments), and it always ends on the exact number the board shows.
Steam
The betting line moving toward a side since morning, usually meaning real money arrived on it.
Contrarian
Backing the side most public tickets are against. Historically the public loses slightly more than it wins.
Notional
The dollar size of a Polymarket trade. Each wallet's bet size versus their own usual is logged today, worth zero points until tested.
Grading
Marking a pick win, loss, or push from the final score. We grade everything ourselves; no source's claimed record is ever trusted.
